      Getting the References Right

      • references should follow the IEEE style/formate guidelines
      The three main parts of a

      reference are as follows:

      • Author’s na me listed as first initial of first name, then full last.
      • Title of article, patent, conference paper, etc., in quotation marks.
      • Title of journal or book in italics.
      • to use the references:
      Each reference number should be enclosed in square brackets on the same line as

      the text, before any punctuation, with a space before the bracket.

      Examples

      “. . .end of the line for my research [13].”

      “The theory was first put forward in 1987 [1].”

      “Scholtz [2] has argued. . . .”

      “For example, see [7].”

      “Several recent studies [3, 4, 15, 22] have suggested that. . . .”

      Note: Authors and dates do not have to be written out after the first reference; use the

      bracketed number. Also, it is not necessary to write “in reference [2].” Just write “in

      [2].”

      The preferred method to cite more than one source at a time is to list each reference in its

      own brackets, then separate with a comma or dash:

      [1], [3], [5]

      [1] – [5]

      Red Herring / Whataboutism example:

      "Why are we even talking about colonizing Mars when we haven't even solved the problem of homelessness/world hunger/x issue on Earth?"

      A counter-argument could point out that progress in space exploration and colonization can provide indirect benefits to humanity that might eventually help solve Earth’s problems:

      • Technological Innovations: Mars colonization could drive new technologies that benefit life on Earth, such as advancements in energy, agriculture, and robotics.
      • Global Cooperation: The international collaboration required for such a massive endeavor could foster peace and shared scientific progress, potentially encouraging cooperation in other areas like poverty alleviation.
      • Long-Term Survival: Colonizing Mars could serve as a long-term survival strategy for humanity, ensuring that human civilization can continue even if Earth faces catastrophic events (natural or man-made).

      By redirecting the conversation to an unrelated issue like homelessness or poverty, the original argument fails to engage with the potential benefits and drawbacks of Mars colonization.

      The argument doesn't explore why Mars colonization and solving homelessness are mutually exclusive projects. In reality, society can (and does) tackle multiple problems at once. Funds for space exploration, for example, often come from budgets unrelated to those for social programs, and success in space exploration doesn’t inherently hinder efforts to address social issues.

      This fallacy assumes that only one problem can be worked on at a time, which is rarely the case in complex, modern societies.

      a literature survey is a survey of existing documents

      • provides a summative overview of what the scholarship says
      • useful to help laypeople to learn about a topic (not for experts)
      • comprehensive but not exhaustive (eg: wikipedia is not the authorities stance on a topic, it is a lit survey)

      a literature review is a review of existing documents

      • seeks to provide a critical reflection on what the scholarship says
      • its useful to help people asses the quality of the argument

      The three techniques covered were, data hardening, control flow checks (CFC) and hybrid methods with watchdogs. Data hardening often requires duplication of computation, either to duplicate variables and operations to compare replicas for a majority vote or duplicate procedure calls [1]. The duplications can either be ran on the same or multiple different computers. The probability that a bit flip would effect all duplicates is very low and worth the additional cost of duplication for fault tolerance against radiation.

      Control Flow Checks (CFC) focus on transient faults on control flow graphs on software. Two common approaches use assertions. ECCA assertions to detect control flow errors by causing divide by 0 errors when an error efects the control flow of a program. YACCA assertion to check for correctness of the current control flow by adding conditional branches.

      Hybrid methods, the techniques used by the preposed architecture, involves using speical hardware modifications called watchdogs. Simple watches run a timer, when the timers are CPU fails to restart them or if there is no activity on the system bus within a given time out: the watchdog triggers an interrupt.The architecture incorporates additional hybrid techniques such as memory partitioning, encoding, and scrubbers to achieve a higher level of reliability.

        1. BRAM (Block RAM) scrubbers repair SEUs in on-chip memory modules (like block RAMs or shift-registers) by periodically reading and rewriting the data to repair any SEU-induced errors.
        1. CRAM (Configuration RAM) scrubbers, on the other hand, repair SEUs in configuration memory (FPGA programming data) by periodically reloading the configuration data from an external non-volatile memory.
        1. BRAM scrubbers are used to prevent accumulation of SEUs in memory modules, while CRAM scrubbers are used to maintain the integrity of the FPGA configuration data and prevent SEU-induced logic or routing errors.
        1. BRAM scrubbing is applied to memory that does not change value very often, while CRAM scrubbing is applied to the entire configuration data of the FPGA.
        1. CRAM scrubbing is more resource-intensive than BRAM scrubbing, as it requires access to an external non-volatile memory and the ability to reload the entire FPGA configuration.
